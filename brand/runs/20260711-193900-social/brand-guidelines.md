# PEP Brand Guidelines — Better Social Drink

For Lou & Shannon / pep-drink.com  
Selected direction: **3 — Better social drink**  
Tagline: **Feel good. Have fun.**

## Positioning

**PEP is a premium protein drink for social, out-of-home moments — a light, refreshing, flavour-led drink people are happy to order when they are out.**

The job of this direction is not to behave like a supplement. It is to make protein feel natural in a bar, dinner, festival, date, rooftop, hotel, or group setting. Nutrition is present, but it should not lead the brand. The emotional promise leads: pleasure, belonging, taste, confidence, and a good time.

### One-liner
**PEP is the premium protein drink for going out: refreshing, flavour-led, social, and made to feel good while having fun.**

### Core idea
People do not order PEP because they are tracking macros. They order it because it feels exciting, premium, refreshing, and right for the moment — with protein as a smart added benefit.

## Who PEP is for

PEP is for people who want a better drink option in social settings:

- People going to bars, dinners, festivals, dates, hotels, rooftops, events, and group hangouts.
- Social drinkers who care about taste, presentation, and occasion fit.
- People who like functional benefits but do not want a gym-coded drink in public.
- Style-conscious consumers who choose drinks partly for how the ritual feels.
- Hospitality customers who want something more interesting than standard soft drinks, water, or basic mixers.

## When they drink it

PEP belongs in moments where the drink is part of the experience:

- At a bar before dinner.
- With friends at a rooftop or terrace.
- At a festival or music event.
- During a date or group dinner.
- At hotels, beach clubs, members’ clubs, and premium casual venues.
- As a refreshing social order when people want something lighter but still fun.

## Approved personality

Use these adjectives as the brand’s centre of gravity:

- social
- refreshing
- premium
- playful
- flavour-led
- experiential
- confident
- aesthetic
- fun
- light
- celebratory
- modern
- smooth
- inviting
- occasion-ready

## Rejected vibes

PEP must not feel like:

- gym-bro protein
- clinical nutrition
- meal replacement
- hardcore sports performance
- medicinal wellness
- moralising abstinence culture
- childish soft drink
- generic health soda
- bargain canned drink
- overly serious luxury
- dense supplement packaging
- sterile tech beverage

## Visual direction

### Design concept
**Fun, premium packaging, vibrant, festival vibe.**

Interpret this as energy and occasion, not as a fixed visual identity. The identity system should feel lively, premium, social, and out-of-home ready. It should have enough confidence for hospitality and enough fun for events.

### Visual mood
Warm, social, high-energy, polished, refreshing, expressive, and premium-but-approachable. The feeling should be: “I would happily hold this at a bar, dinner, or festival.”

### Photography direction
Show PEP in real social rituals:

- Hands holding cans or serves in group settings.
- Tables, bars, terraces, hotel counters, festivals, dates, and dinners.
- Close-ups that make the drink feel chilled, flavour-led, and refreshing.
- People enjoying the moment rather than performing fitness.
- Founders and brand world should feel warm, sociable, and contemporary.

Avoid isolated supplement-style product shots as the main brand world. Product clarity matters, but the occasion must carry the story.

## Visual do / don’t

### Do

- Build around social context and drink ritual.
- Make flavour and refreshment highly visible in hierarchy.
- Keep the protein benefit present but secondary.
- Use layouts that feel premium, confident, and easy to understand quickly.
- Allow expressive energy, movement, and celebration.
- Make the can feel suitable for hospitality, events, and social ordering.
- Create a system that can expand across Coconut Mango and future flavours without making one flavour family define the brand.

### Don’t

- Lead with protein grams as the main emotional hook.
- Use gym, muscle, shaker, macro, or transformation cues.
- Make the brand look like a powder supplement or meal replacement.
- Make the pack too clinical, too sparse, or too rational.
- Make the brand feel juvenile or candy-like.
- Position the brand mainly around what it is not; position it around the better social moment.
- Copy competitor logos, label structures, or signature assets.

## Copy direction

### Tone
Playful, confident, social, crisp, and flavour-forward. Copy should sound like a good host: upbeat, tasteful, and easy to join.

### Copy should say

- “Order this when you’re out.”
- “Feel good. Have fun.”
- “A refreshing protein drink for social moments.”
- “Light, flavour-led, and made for going out.”
- “Protein, without the gym-drink feeling.”

### Copy should avoid

- Macro-counting language as the main hook.
- Fitness transformation promises.
- Clinical supplement claims.
- Heavy wellness jargon.
- Moralising social-drinking language.
- Over-explaining protein in a defensive way.

## Brand peer split

### In-line brands: strict fit
These brands are closest because they share the social-drink mindset and broadly comparable ready-to-serve or occasion-led beverage format.

- **Kin Euphorics** — social, experiential, mood-led drink world. Borrow the sense of ritual and occasion, not the exact look.
- **Royal Club Mocktails** — adult social serve, flavour-forward ordering logic, hospitality relevance.
- **On The Rocks Cocktails** — premium ready-to-serve cocktail energy, bar-inspired confidence, strong flavour hierarchy.

### Positioning fit, type mismatch
These brands fit the social occasion and can inspire the brand world, but their core drink format is less directly comparable to PEP’s protein drink format.

- **Lyre’s** — strong premium social ritual and sophisticated serve culture, but primarily a spirits-style format.
- **Seedlip** — premium mixed-drink ritual and hospitality credibility, but primarily a spirits-style format.
- **Rekorderlig** — strong outdoor, festival, and social refreshment cues, but cider is a different format.
- **Royal Club Ginger Ale** — adult mixer and social serve cues, but mixer logic is different from a standalone protein drink.
- **Ole cocktails** — celebratory RTD energy and flavour-led hierarchy, but its cocktail format and lifestyle-upgrade lane are not PEP’s core.

### Other-positioning peers
These are useful for category awareness, but they should not drive this direction.

- Functional protein and hydration brands are too rational, gym-oriented, or performance-led.
- Lifestyle wellness sodas and kombuchas are closer to everyday wellness habits than to high-energy social ordering.
- Prebiotic sodas are useful for understanding modern health beverages, but PEP should not become a generic gut-health lifestyle soda.

## Brand fit rules

1. If the reference feels made for bars, dinners, festivals, dates, rooftops, hotels, or group rituals, it may fit.
2. If the reference leads with gym performance, macros, recovery, or discipline, reject it for this direction.
3. If the design makes taste, occasion, and social confidence feel more important than nutrition claims, it fits.
4. Protein is a benefit, not the brand’s main personality.
5. Alcohol content is not the sorting axis; judge references by occasion, drink format, and brand behaviour.
6. Premium should feel fun and accessible, not stiff or exclusionary.
7. Flavour should be prominent, but no single flavour family should define the brand because the lineup will grow.
8. Copy should sound like an invitation, not a supplement label.
9. Packaging should feel credible in hospitality and exciting in hand.
10. Never confuse PEP from pep-drink.com with drinkpep.com.

## Anti-references

- **Gym-bro protein RTDs** — too muscular, functional, and performance-coded for a social drink.
- **Clinical supplement brands** — too rational and health-problem oriented.
- **Meal replacement drinks** — too utilitarian and solo-consumption oriented.
- **drinkpep.com** — name collision only; different product world and not the same brand.
- **Kids’ juice / candy soda cues** — too juvenile for premium social occasions.

## Final strategic guardrail

PEP should make protein feel like it belongs in the fun part of life. If an idea makes PEP feel like a supplement you tolerate, reject it. If it makes PEP feel like a drink you want to order, hold, share, and enjoy when you are out, it fits.
