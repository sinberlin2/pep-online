# PEP Brand Guidelines — Better Social Drink

For Lou & Shannon / pep-drink.com

## Positioning choice
PEP is being built as a Better social drink, not a gym protein tool and not a vague everyday wellness soda.

Core idea: PEP is the premium protein drink people want to order when they are out: light, refreshing, fruity, alcohol-free, and made for social moments.

Tagline: Feel good. Have fun.

## Who PEP is for
- Social adults who want a more exciting alcohol-free option in bars, restaurants, hotels, festivals, dates, dinners and rooftop settings.
- People who like the ritual and aesthetics of ordering a special drink, but do not want alcohol or a boring soft drink.
- Flavour-first consumers who care about feeling good, but do not want the night to become about macros, gym goals or discipline.
- Friend groups and hosts who want a drink that feels inclusive, premium and fun.

## When they drink it
- At a bar when they want something more special than a soft drink.
- At dinner when they want an alcohol-free order that still feels adult and exciting.
- At festivals, rooftops, hotels and events where the drink is part of the experience.
- On dates and group nights out where taste, image and ritual matter.
- As a refreshing social alternative that happens to include protein, not as a protein task.

## What PEP must feel like
Approved adjectives: fun, premium, social, experiential, refreshing, fruity, aesthetic, celebratory, inclusive, light, playful, flavour-led, confident.

PEP should feel like something people want to be seen holding, ordering and sharing. The protein benefit should create a feel-good reason to believe, but the emotional hook is pleasure, belonging and experience.

## Rejected vibes
- Gym-bro protein, bodybuilding, performance-maxing or post-workout identity.
- Clinical supplement, hydration sachet, electrolyte science or vitamin utility.
- Overly calm wellness minimalism that feels like a desk drink rather than a night-out drink.
- Serious heritage spirits imitation that becomes too formal, old-world or bartender-only.
- Kids’ soda, juice-box sweetness, cartoon energy or cheap party cues.
- Alcohol-heavy behaviour or booze imitation that conflicts with alcohol-free social ritual.

## Visual do and do not
Do:
- Use the active design concept: fun, premium packaging, vibrant, festival vibe, fruity.
- Prioritise social scenes, hands, groups, tables, serving rituals and out-of-home energy.
- Make flavours feel desirable and easy to choose.
- Keep nutrition present but secondary.
- Make the brand feel premium-but-approachable: suitable for a bar menu, a hotel fridge, a festival stand or a dinner table.

Do not:
- Lead with protein claims as if this is a sports nutrition product.
- Use dense supplement-style claim hierarchy.
- Build the world around gym bags, shaker bottles, workout mirrors or athletic recovery.
- Copy non-alcoholic spirits brands directly; PEP is not pretending to be a spirit.
- Make the product feel medicinal, restrictive or preachy about alcohol.

## Copy do and do not
Do:
- Lead with occasion, taste and feeling: order it, share it, enjoy it.
- Use short, confident, social language.
- Make the alcohol-free benefit feel positive and exciting, not apologetic.
- Mention protein as a feel-good bonus: enough to make the choice feel smart, never the whole personality.
- Keep the tone playful, premium and socially fluent.

Do not:
- Use gym language like gains, shred, bulk, anabolic or post-workout.
- Use clinical stacks language like electrolyte stack or nootropic stack.
- Moralise with guilt-free, cheat day, cleanse or detox language.
- Over-explain nutrition in hero copy.
- Sound like drinkpep.com, which is a separate US energy/probiotic brand and not Lou & Shannon’s PEP.

## Reference split
### In-line brands — strict fit for this direction
Kin Euphorics, Lyre’s and Seedlip are the strongest strategic peers because they build social, alcohol-free drinking rituals with high branding importance and hospitality relevance.

### Positioning fit but type mismatch
Everleaf, Ish, Ole cocktails, Yaya Kombucha, Walker Brothers and Protochol can teach parts of the occasion, ritual or event language, but each has a drink-format mismatch. Use them only for specific learnings, never as direct templates.

### Other positioning peers
HOLY, LMNT, NOCCO and Vitamin Well belong mainly to functional protein or hydration. BLOOM, OLIPOP, Poppi, Alive & Well, Recess and more fiz belong mainly to lifestyle wellness. They are useful for contrast, not for PEP’s active direction.

## Mechanical brand fit rules
- If the idea makes PEP feel like a drink people order when they are out, it likely fits.
- If the idea makes PEP feel like a gym supplement, reject it.
- If the first message is protein, reject or demote it.
- If the first message is flavour, social ritual or occasion, keep exploring.
- If the work could sit naturally in a bar, restaurant, hotel or festival context, it fits.
- If it only makes sense in a gym, office routine or wellness shelf, it belongs to another direction.
- If alcohol-free feels like a positive choice, it fits.
- If alcohol-free feels like restriction, compromise or moralising, reject it.
- If the brand feels premium but still fun, it fits.
- If it feels either too clinical or too childish, reject it.
