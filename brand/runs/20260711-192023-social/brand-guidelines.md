# PEP Brand Guidelines — Better Social Drink

## Direction
**Selected positioning:** Better social drink  
**Domain:** pep-drink.com  
**Founders:** Lou & Shannon  
**Tagline:** Feel good. Have fun.

PEP is a premium protein drink for social, out-of-home moments. It should feel like something people are excited to order at a bar, dinner, rooftop, festival, date, hotel, or restaurant — not like a gym supplement that accidentally appeared in nightlife.

## One-line positioning
PEP is the refreshing premium protein drink made for people who want their going-out drink to feel fun, fruity, elevated, and worth ordering.

## Who PEP is for
PEP is for socially active, taste-led people who care about how a drink feels in the moment: the serve, the flavour, the setting, the photo, the table, the group energy. They may like wellness, but they are not shopping this product primarily for macros. They want a drink that feels exciting, premium, and easy to say yes to.

## When they drink it
- At bars, restaurants, hotels, festivals, rooftop events, and dinners.
- During dates, group nights out, beach clubs, terraces, parties, and pre-event meetups.
- When they want a drink that feels more special than a basic soft drink.
- When they want refreshment, flavour, and social ritual without heaviness.
- When they want the protein benefit to feel clever and light, not clinical.

## Core strategic tension
The main risk is that **protein can feel strange in a social context**. PEP must solve this by leading with pleasure, flavour, occasion, and ritual first. Protein should be present as a smart product truth, but it should not dominate the emotional story.

## Approved adjectives
Use these as the brand’s emotional guardrails:
- social
- fun
- premium
- refreshing
- fruity
- experiential
- playful
- elevated
- confident
- modern
- flavour-led
- celebratory
- aesthetic
- light
- feel-good

## Rejected vibes
Avoid anything that makes PEP feel like:
- gym-bro protein
- clinical nutrition
- meal replacement
- hardcore performance fuel
- electrolyte sachet culture
- medicinal wellness
- children’s juice
- bargain soft drink
- generic health soda
- overly serious luxury
- preachy moderation culture
- copycat drinkpep.com energy/probiotic positioning

## Visual strategy do’s
- Make the pack and website feel like a drink people would proudly hold in a social setting.
- Prioritise occasion energy: hands, glasses, serves, tables, groups, music, movement, terraces, events, and shared moments.
- Use flavour as a major hierarchy driver, especially for Coconut Mango and the five upcoming flavours.
- Keep layouts premium but not stiff: enough space to feel elevated, enough energy to feel alive.
- Make product education fast and effortless: what it is, what it tastes like, when to drink it, and why protein belongs here.
- Treat PEP as a social ritual, not a supplement routine.

## Visual strategy don’ts
- Do not use gym poses, shaker bottles, flexing, performance charts, or supplement-style comparison panels.
- Do not make nutrition claims the first emotional impression.
- Do not make the brand feel like a pharmacy, clinic, or sports nutrition aisle.
- Do not copy competitor logos, labels, illustration systems, or signature brand assets.
- Do not make the design too minimal, quiet, or wellness-neutral for this direction; it needs enough social energy to work in hospitality.
- Do not frame the brand around an alcohol stance. The organising idea is the social occasion, not what other drinks contain.

## Copy do’s
- Lead with taste, feeling, and occasion.
- Keep copy short, confident, and easy to say out loud.
- Make protein feel like a pleasant surprise: a reason to feel good, not a lecture.
- Use lines that suggest social momentum: ordering, sharing, arriving, staying out, having fun.
- Use the fixed tagline: **Feel good. Have fun.**

## Copy don’ts
- Do not sound like a supplement label.
- Do not overuse macro language.
- Do not shame other choices.
- Do not describe PEP primarily as a replacement for another category.
- Do not lean on abstinence, detox, discipline, or restriction language.
- Do not confuse PEP with drinkpep.com, the separate US energy/probiotic brand.

## Brand peer split

### In-line brands — strict fit
These brands share the active social positioning and a close enough drink format or ordering context to help define PEP’s world.

- **Kin Euphorics** — strong social experience framing, mood-led ritual, premium out-of-home feel.
- **Royal Club Mocktails** — adult social serve, cocktail-inspired ordering context, flavour-led presentation.
- **On The Rocks Cocktails** — premium ready-to-drink social serve, bar-inspired cues, event and hospitality relevance.

### Positioning fit, type mismatch
These brands nail useful parts of the social-drink world, but their core format differs from PEP’s protein drink format. They are design inspiration, not direct templates.

- **Lyre’s** — strong elevated social ritual and premium serve culture, but spirit-style format is less direct.
- **Seedlip** — sophisticated mixology and occasion cues, but used more as an ingredient/serve builder than a ready protein drink.
- **Rekorderlig** — fruit-forward social drinking and festival/terrace relevance, but cider is a different core format.
- **Royal Club Ginger Ale** — adult mixer codes and hospitality relevance, but mixer format is less direct.

### Peer brands from other positionings
These brands belong mainly to functional or lifestyle directions, not the Better Social Drink direction. They can inform category awareness but should not set the PEP brand brain.

Functional peers to avoid becoming: HOLY, LMNT, NOCCO, PROTOCHOL, Vitamin Well, Juuz.  
Lifestyle/wellness peers to avoid over-blending with: Bloom, Ever Tree, Olipop, Poppi, Alive & Well, Recess, Ole Cocktails, Ish, Yaya Kombucha, Walker Brothers.

## Mechanical brand-fit rules
1. If the idea makes PEP feel more like something ordered while out with friends, it is likely on-strategy.
2. If the idea makes PEP feel more like a supplement routine, it is off-strategy.
3. Lead with flavour, serve, and social feeling before nutrition.
4. Protein is a smart benefit, not the emotional headline.
5. Do not sort references by alcohol content; sort them by occasion, ritual, and drink format.
6. Direct peers are social drinks with premium ordering energy.
7. Type-mismatch peers can inspire occasion, layout, and serve culture, but not PEP’s product promise.
8. Lifestyle wellness references are secondary; use only if they do not soften the social energy too much.
9. Functional fitness references are mostly anti-direction unless Lou & Shannon explicitly mark them in-line.
10. Never treat drinkpep.com as the same brand or product.
