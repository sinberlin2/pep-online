# PEP Brand Guidelines — Functional Protein Drink

## Active positioning

**Direction:** Functional protein drink  
**Domain:** pep-drink.com  
**Founders:** Lou & Shannon  
**Tagline:** Feel good. Have fun.

PEP should be built as a premium, light and refreshing protein drink for people who want to hit their protein goals without the heaviness, effort, or meal-replacement feeling of traditional protein formats.

This direction is functional first: PEP is a useful nutrition tool, not primarily a lifestyle accessory or alcohol alternative. It can still feel enjoyable, premium, and out-of-home friendly, but the first buying logic is: **“Is this useful for my routine?”**

## Positioning statement

PEP helps active, routine-minded people get convenient protein in a light, refreshing drink they can enjoy after training, between meals, at work, or on the go.

## Who PEP is for

- People who struggle to consistently hit protein targets.
- Gym-goers, runners, wellness studio customers, and active professionals.
- People who want protein without a thick shake, heavy bar, or overly intense sports-drink feeling.
- Customers who care strongly about nutrition, still expect good taste, and want a drink that feels premium enough for cafés and out-of-home routines.
- People looking for a repeatable habit: something they can drink several times per week.

## When they drink it

- After the gym.
- After a run or fitness class.
- Between meals.
- At work or during a focused desk break.
- On the commute from training to the next part of the day.
- In gyms, wellness studios, healthy cafés, and grab-and-go fridges.

## Approved brand adjectives

Use these to guide copy, page structure, content, and creative direction:

- functional
- protein-rich
- light
- refreshing
- convenient
- efficient
- disciplined
- clean
- health-focused
- routine-friendly
- premium-but-practical
- energised, without becoming an energy-drink brand
- enjoyable, without becoming a treat-only brand

## Rejected vibes

Reject anything that makes PEP feel like:

- hardcore gym-bro protein
- bodybuilding supplement culture
- aggressive performance fuel
- meal replacement
- medicinal nutrition
- sugary soda with a protein claim attached
- vague wellness with no functional reason to buy
- alcohol replacement as the main proposition
- party-first, festival-first, or cocktail-first positioning
- kids’ juice, novelty drink, or unserious gimmick
- drinkpep.com or any US energy/probiotic product using the same name

## Visual strategy

### Design concept

**Clean.**

The visual system should make PEP feel simple to understand, purposeful, premium, and easy to trust. The first read should communicate the protein benefit and refreshing format quickly. The overall feel should be uncluttered, confident, and modern without becoming cold or clinical.

### Visual mood

- Light in feel, but nutritionally credible.
- Premium, but not precious.
- Active, but not extreme.
- Refreshing, but not soda-like.
- Useful, but not medical.
- Routine-friendly, with enough enjoyment to support the tagline: “Feel good. Have fun.”

### Photography direction

Show real, repeatable usage moments rather than exaggerated fitness performance:

- Post-session recovery.
- Walking out of a studio or gym.
- Desk break with a functional snack alternative.
- Commuting after exercise.
- Healthy café or fridge context.
- Product in hand, on the move, or integrated into a normal active day.

Avoid overly staged gym flexing, extreme workout intensity, nightclub cues, cocktail rituals, or wellness clichés with no product function.

## Copy strategy

### Copy should do

- Lead with usefulness: protein, convenience, lightness, refreshment.
- Make the benefit easy to understand in one scan.
- Treat PEP as a repeatable habit, not an occasional indulgence.
- Keep language direct, upbeat, and grounded.
- Support rational buying logic while keeping the brand enjoyable.
- Use the tagline as a human counterweight to functional claims.

### Copy should not do

- Overpromise body transformation.
- Sound like a supplement label only.
- Use macho, punishing, or elite-athlete language.
- Make social drinking the main reason to buy.
- Lead with abstract lifestyle identity before explaining the protein role.
- Copy competitor claims, structures, names, or logo ideas.

## Competitive reference split

### In-line brand — strict fit

**HOLY** is the clearest in-line peer because it sits closest to the functional protein and vitamin-enhanced drink lane. Use it only as a strategic reference for benefit clarity, functional credibility, and repeat-use framing. Do not copy its identity, packaging, logo, or exact claim architecture.

### Positioning fit, type mismatch

These brands share useful functional cues but are not strict PEP references because their drink format or category is different:

- **LMNT:** Strong functional clarity and routine logic, but it is hydration/electrolyte-led rather than protein-led.
- **NOCCO:** Strong fitness and recovery relevance, but it is BCAA/energy-led rather than a light protein drink.
- **Vitamin Well:** Strong functional hydration clarity, but lacks the protein-first proposition.
- **PROTOCHOL:** Contains protein, but the alcohol fusion and novelty angle conflict with PEP’s functional routine role.
- **more fiz:** Functional soda cues are useful, but the lifestyle soda frame is weaker than a protein target frame.

### Other positioning peers — not active direction

Lifestyle upgrade brands like Bloom, Olipop, Poppi, Recess, Alive & Well, Ish, and related wellness beverages should not define PEP in this direction. They can be useful market context, but they over-index on lifestyle identity, treat-like refreshment, gut health, calm, or social wellness rather than protein utility.

Social drink brands like Kin Euphorics, Lyre’s, Seedlip, and premium mocktail/aperitif references should not define PEP in this direction. They make sense for the “better social drink” route, but functional protein must not become cocktail replacement, bar ritual, or mood-enhancing social experience.

## Mechanical brand fit rules

1. If the protein benefit is not clear immediately, the concept fails this positioning.
2. If the main occasion is a bar, festival, dinner party, or alcohol replacement, reject it for this direction.
3. If the language sounds like bodybuilding, extreme performance, or supplement culture, reject it.
4. If the design feels cluttered, gimmicky, or novelty-led, reject it.
5. If the proposition could apply equally to any wellness soda, it is too vague.
6. If taste is presented without function, strengthen the protein and lightness message.
7. If function is presented without enjoyment, bring back refreshment and the tagline.
8. Use out-of-home routine contexts such as gyms, studios, workdays, and healthy cafés; do not make social nightlife the core context.
9. Treat competitors as strategic references only; never copy logos, identity systems, or distinctive assets.
10. Never treat drinkpep.com as the same brand or product.
