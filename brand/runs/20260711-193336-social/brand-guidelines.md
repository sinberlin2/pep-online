# PEP Brand Guidelines — Better Social Drink

## Positioning
PEP is a premium protein drink for social, out-of-home moments: bars, dinners, festivals, dates, rooftops, hotels and events. It should feel like something people actively want to order when they are out — not a nutrition task, not a gym product, and not a compromise.

**Positioning statement:** PEP gives social drinkers a light, refreshing, flavour-led protein drink that feels premium, fun and made for the moment.

**Tagline:** Feel good. Have fun.

## Who PEP is for
- People who want a more interesting drink option when they go out.
- Social, aesthetic, experience-led drinkers who choose with mood, setting and identity in mind.
- Hospitality customers who want something that feels grown-up, premium and exciting without defaulting to standard soft drinks.
- People who like wellness benefits, but do not want a wellness lecture in a bar or festival setting.

## When they drink it
- With friends before or during a night out.
- At bars, restaurants, terraces, rooftops, festivals, beach clubs, hotels and events.
- On dates, at dinners, during daytime parties and after-work drinks.
- Whenever the decision is: “What do I want to order that feels good and fun?”

## Strategic role of protein
Protein is a credibility layer, not the headline personality. In this direction, PEP must not behave like a supplement brand. The product benefit can appear as a smart plus — 7g protein, 49 calories, gluten-free — but the emotional sell is taste, social ritual and premium refreshment.

## Approved personality adjectives
Premium, social, refreshing, flavour-led, playful, elevated, experiential, celebratory, confident, aesthetic, welcoming, smooth, distinctive, modern, fun.

## Rejected vibes
- Gym-bro, sports-performance, gains-focused.
- Clinical, supplement-like, electrolyte-mix seriousness.
- Diet-coded, restrictive, moralising or optimisation-heavy.
- Meal-replacement heaviness.
- Childish soft-drink energy.
- Abstinence-led or preachy social-drink framing.
- Overly quiet lifestyle wellness that feels more like a desk drink than an order-at-the-bar drink.

## Visual direction
**Design concept:** fun, premium packaging, vibrant, festival vibe.

The identity should feel high-energy, premium and social. It can be expressive, confident and occasion-led, but it must still feel polished enough for hospitality. Think clear shelf impact, strong flavour hierarchy, memorable serve moments, and layouts that feel designed for photos, menus and social settings.

### Visual do
- Lead with social occasion energy: groups, serves, movement, hospitality and events.
- Make flavour and refreshment immediately desirable.
- Keep protein visible but secondary.
- Use premium packaging behaviour: confident hierarchy, intentional spacing, polished details, and strong recognition at a distance.
- Show PEP as something you order, hold, share and photograph.

### Visual don’t
- Do not make the pack look like a gym RTD, shaker supplement, energy drink or medical nutrition product.
- Do not over-index on nutrition badges, macro panels or functional claims.
- Do not build the brand around one flavour family; the range will grow.
- Do not copy competitor logos, layouts or distinctive assets.
- Do not reference drinkpep.com; it is a different US energy/probiotic brand.

## Voice direction
PEP copy should be short, confident, social and sensorial. It should sound like a drink people want to order, not a health product people need to justify.

### Copy do
- Use language around taste, refreshment, serve, ritual, feeling and occasion.
- Keep nutrition claims factual and compact.
- Make the drink feel fun without becoming childish.
- Use lines that would work on menus, event signage and social posts.

### Copy don’t
- Do not lead with macros, gym goals or body optimisation.
- Do not use shame, restriction or “better than you” wellness language.
- Do not position the brand primarily around abstinence.
- Do not describe the full brand as one specific taste direction.

## Brand peer split
### In-line brands
These are closest because they share the social-drink occasion and a broadly ready-to-drink or soft-drink/mixer format.

### Positioning fit, type mismatch
These brands are useful design inspiration because they nail the social ritual, premium serve culture or going-out energy, but their core drink format is not PEP’s protein refreshment format.

### Other positioning peers
These brands belong mainly to functional protein or lifestyle wellness. They may teach useful category lessons, but they are not the core PEP social direction.

## Mechanical brand-fit rules
1. If the primary moment is “I order this when I’m out,” it fits.
2. If the primary moment is gym, desk, recovery, hydration routine or meal replacement, it does not fit.
3. Taste, serve and social ritual must lead; protein must support.
4. Do not classify peers by alcohol content. Classify by occasion, mindset and drink format.
5. Cocktail, cider and spirit brands can inspire premium social cues, but are type-mismatch references if the format is not close to PEP’s protein drink.
6. The brand must feel fun and premium at once.
7. Avoid locking the identity to one flavour family.
8. Never treat drinkpep.com as the same product or brand universe.
