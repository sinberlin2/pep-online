# PEP Brand Guidelines — Better Social Drink

## Direction
**Selected positioning:** Better social drink  
**Domain:** pep-drink.com  
**Founders:** Lou & Shannon  
**Tagline:** Feel good. Have fun.

PEP should be built as a premium protein drink for social, out-of-home moments — not as a gym shake, not as a medical function drink, and not as the unrelated drinkpep.com product.

## Positioning statement
PEP is the better social drink for people who want something exciting to order when they are out: light, refreshing, flavour-led, protein-powered, and made for moments with other people.

## Who PEP is for
- People who want a better drink option at bars, restaurants, hotels, festivals, dinners, dates, and rooftop events.
- Social drinkers who want the ritual and pleasure of ordering something special without defaulting to alcohol or boring soft drinks.
- Aesthetic, experience-led consumers who care about taste, occasion, and how the drink feels in their hand and in the moment.
- People open to protein as a feel-good benefit, but not looking for a gym product in social settings.

## When they drink it
- As a first drink when arriving somewhere social.
- During a dinner, date, party, festival, hotel stay, or rooftop event.
- When they want to stay fresh but still feel part of the occasion.
- When they want a fruity, premium, refreshing drink that feels more exciting than a standard soft drink.

## Core idea
PEP should make protein feel socially desirable. The product benefit is real, but the brand should lead with pleasure, taste, ritual, and belonging. Nutrition supports the story; it should not dominate the story.

## Approved personality adjectives
social, experiential, premium, fun, fruity, refreshing, aesthetic, sophisticated, inclusive, playful, celebratory, confident, adult, balanced, distinctive, feel-good

## Rejected vibes
- Gym-bro, macro-counting, performance-first, muscle-building.
- Clinical, medicinal, supplement-like, overly technical.
- Childish soft drink energy.
- Generic wellness vagueness.
- Alcohol-copycat without a PEP point of view.
- Overly serious luxury that loses the fun.

## Visual strategy do’s
- Make the product feel like something people want to be seen ordering.
- Prioritise premium-but-fun packaging energy.
- Use generous, confident layouts rather than cluttered supplement-style information.
- Let flavour and occasion drive the first impression.
- Create a sense of social ritual: serve, toast, share, arrive, celebrate.
- Keep protein present but secondary to the social drinking experience.

## Visual strategy don’ts
- Do not make the pack feel like a sports nutrition product.
- Do not lead with protein numbers as the main emotional hook.
- Do not use dense claim stacks, technical badges, or gym-coded composition.
- Do not imitate non-alcoholic spirit labels too directly; PEP is not a faux spirit.
- Do not make the brand feel flat, quiet, or purely everyday wellness.

## Copy do’s
- Lead with occasion: out, together, nights, dates, dinners, festivals, first rounds, staying fresh.
- Use short, confident, social lines.
- Make the protein feel like a feel-good twist, not a lecture.
- Keep taste language juicy, refreshing, and craveable.
- Use the tagline consistently: **Feel good. Have fun.**

## Copy don’ts
- Do not sound like a supplement label.
- Do not over-explain nutrition.
- Do not use gym transformation language.
- Do not imply PEP is the same as drinkpep.com.
- Do not frame PEP as a punishment, restriction, detox, or compromise.

## Competitive split
### In-line brands
These are the closest active-direction peers because they compete for social drinking ritual, hospitality ordering, adult non-alcoholic choice, and premium occasion value.

- Kin Euphorics — strong mood-led social drink peer.
- Lyre’s — strong non-alcoholic social ritual peer.
- Seedlip — premium alcohol-free social drinking pioneer.
- Royal Club Mocktails — direct non-alcoholic mocktail occasion peer.
- Royal Club Ginger Ale — adult mixer and bar-order peer.

### Positioning fit but type mismatch
These brands are useful inspiration for occasion, ritual, or flavour energy, but should not define PEP because the drink format or core promise differs.

- Rekorderlig — strong fruit-led social refreshment, but cider is not PEP’s protein-led alcohol-free proposition.
- Ole cocktails — festive ready-to-drink energy, but cocktail format and alcohol association are not PEP’s core.
- Ish — premium mindful drinking fit, but imitation spirit/cocktail logic is not PEP’s own lane.
- Ever Tree — botanical social sophistication, but aperitif/soft drink format is weaker as a protein reference.
- Walker Brothers — craft social alternative, but kombucha/fermentation would pull PEP toward wellness craft.
- Yaya Kombucha — premium refreshment and craft cues, but kombucha is a different mindset and product promise.

## Brand fit rules
1. If it feels more like a gym drink than a drink to order while out, reject it.
2. If the first message is nutrition rather than social pleasure, rewrite it.
3. If it feels childish, cheap, clinical, or purely functional, reject it.
4. If it could sit naturally in bars, restaurants, hotels, and festivals, it is likely on direction.
5. If protein feels like the whole story, reduce it; if protein disappears entirely, restore it as the feel-good twist.
6. If the brand feels like a copy of a spirit, mocktail, or cider brand, add a clearer PEP point of view.
7. Keep every expression aligned with: **Feel good. Have fun.**
